Framework for the Decision
Three questions govern every decision:

1. Does the experiment resolve a genuine scientific ambiguity, or does it only satisfy a reviewer's rhetorical expectation?
Some weaknesses are validity threats — without the experiment, a key claim could be wrong. Others are presentation problems — the science is fine but the paper creates an impression it can't fully support. The former warrant experimental effort; the latter are better handled in prose.

2. Does the data for the experiment already exist?
Several SDPI suite outputs (Stage 53 eta_geom, Stage 39 centroid gaps, per-method α table) contain exactly the measurements needed for fixes W4 and W5. Running analysis on existing data costs hours, not weeks. These are near-free upgrades.

3. What is the risk if the experiment goes the wrong way?
W3 (WavCaps-on-WavCaps evaluation) is the highest-risk experiment in the list. If it shows that WavCaps-trained models perform comparably to AudioCaps-trained models when evaluated on WavCaps audio, the paper's central finding shifts from "caption quality is the bottleneck" to "distribution shift is the bottleneck." That is a scientifically important result but would require significantly rewriting the paper. Running W3 is necessary precisely because the stakes are high — but that is also a reason to go in with open eyes.

Fix-by-Fix Analysis
W1 — Single modality triple (law vs conjecture)
The scientific issue: A law validated on one triple is a scaling relationship found in one system. The geometric mean form is theoretically motivated, but so are several alternative forms (arithmetic mean, raw product, harmonic mean). The fact that the geometric mean fits r²=0.823 on a controlled experimental program does not distinguish "this functional form is correct for text-anchored transitivity" from "this functional form fit the data well in this particular setup." Validation requires at least one external test — a new modality triple where α, av_it, av_at, and av_ia are all independently measurable.

The experimental fix in practice: A second triple requires a pretrained encoder for the new modality, a text-paired training corpus, a training run, and a held-out evaluation. For depth: a DPT encoder is freely available, SUN RGBD has depth-caption-style annotations, and the Phase A/Phase B protocol transfers directly. Effort estimate: 2–3 weeks including environment setup, data preprocessing, training runs, and debugging. For video: VideoMAE or InternVideo as encoder, WebVid or HowTo100M for video-text, similar timeline. If the law holds on the second triple (similar functional form, plausible α, same bottleneck structure), the paper's title is justified.

The written fix in practice: Replace "The Bottleneck Law" with "An Empirical Bottleneck Relationship" or "A Bottleneck Scaling Conjecture." Move the equation from a numbered display to an inline fit. Add to the abstract: "We derive and validate this relationship for the image-text-audio triple; whether the geometric mean form and the α decomposition generalize to other modality pairs is an open and directly testable prediction." Add to limitations: "The primary limitation of this analysis is single-triple validation. The two theoretical frameworks (symmetry argument, Cauchy-Schwarz ceiling) motivate the geometric mean form but do not constitute a proof, and the r²=0.823 fit is derived from the same data used to motivate the form. Cross-triple validation is the appropriate next test."

Decision: Written fix is necessary; experimental fix is high-value if timeline permits.

A rebranding from "law" to "conjecture" or "scaling relationship" is not optional — NeurIPS reviewers will flag a single-triple law paper. The written fix is necessary regardless of whether you also run the experiment. If this is pre-submission and there are several weeks available, running a second modality triple (depth is the lowest-friction option) would transform the paper. The contribution would go from "we observe this pattern in one system" to "we propose and validate this law across modality types." That difference is significant for acceptance probability at a top venue. If the timeline is a rebuttal window (typically 7–10 days), the experimental fix is not feasible and the written fix must hold.

W2 — Encoder confound in the headline result
The scientific issue: The linear probe (av_ia=0.0462) uses CLAP features; the joint_clip_head reference (av_ia=0.0424) uses a CLIP-style audio head trained jointly with image-audio-text triplets. These are different audio encoders, so the 9% advantage of the linear probe over the joint reference is fully explainable by CLAP being a better audio encoder than the CLIP audio head, with no contribution from training protocol. The paper acknowledges this but frames it as "the supervision gap can disappear," which is technically true but misleading — the supervision gap disappears because you switched to a better encoder, not because the modular protocol is as powerful as joint training.

The experimental fix in practice: Train a joint reference using CLAP audio features in a joint image-audio-text InfoNCE loss. This is the encoder-matched comparison the paper currently lacks. Cost: one training run (5 seeds × same hyperparameters as modular training), roughly 1–2 days. The result tells you the true comparison: if joint CLAP > linear probe CLAP, then modular training cannot match joint training even with the same encoder, and the paper's claim needs softening. If joint CLAP ≈ linear probe CLAP, then the modular protocol genuinely matches joint training at matched encoder quality, which is a strong and valid result.

The written fix in practice: One careful paragraph in Section 4: "The linear probe uses CLAP audio features while joint_clip_head uses CLIP-style audio features trained jointly; this encoder difference prevents a direct method comparison. The appropriate reading of Table 1's modular row is an upper bound on modular performance using state-of-the-art audio features, not evidence of method equivalence with joint training. The claim is that, given a strong audio encoder, the zero-supervision modular protocol can reach the performance level of a weaker encoder trained with full supervision — a meaningful practical result." Remove the phrase "the supervision gap can disappear" from the abstract and conclusion; replace with "the performance gap between modular and joint-trained systems is substantially reduced when encoder quality is matched."

Decision: Written fix is sufficient, but the experimental fix would genuinely strengthen the paper.

The encoder confound is already acknowledged in the paper. The written fix is just making the acknowledgment more prominent and precise — this is a rephrasing task, not a scientific gap. However, the question of whether joint CLAP training outperforms modular CLAP training is genuinely interesting and not answered by the current experiments. If the experimental fix shows that modular ≈ joint even at matched encoder quality, that would be a strong result worth adding to Table 1. Cost is 1–2 days; benefit is high. This is probably the best cost-to-benefit ratio among all the experimental fixes.

W3 — Train-eval distribution shift confound
The scientific issue: Stage 45 trains Phase B on WavCaps audio and evaluates on AudioCaps audio. WavCaps audio clips come from BBC Sound Effects, Freesound, and AudioSet SL; AudioCaps clips are a specific YouTube-sourced set. The acoustic distribution, clip length, recording quality, and event type distributions differ significantly between these sources. The measured av_at gap (Δ≈−0.11) between AudioCaps-trained and WavCaps-trained Phase B is claimed to be caused by caption quality (CLAP margin −56%), but could equally be caused by the WavCaps audio clips being acoustically dissimilar to the AudioCaps evaluation clips. A model trained on birdsong clips from BBC Sound Effects may simply not learn the right audio representations for the YouTube amateur recordings in AudioCaps — regardless of caption quality.

The Stage 38 evidence helps but does not resolve this: Stage 38 shows that WavCaps real captions have a −56% CLAP alignment margin compared to AudioCaps captions. This is valid evidence that WavCaps caption quality is lower. However, the CLAP model used to measure this margin is trained on AudioCaps-adjacent data, making it potentially biased against WavCaps captions. More importantly, Stage 38 measures caption quality independently of retrieval evaluation — it is indirect evidence for the bottleneck claim, not a direct measurement of how much of the retrieval gap is attributable to quality vs shift.

The experimental fix in practice: This requires evaluating WavCaps-trained models on a WavCaps-held-out test split. Since WavCaps doesn't have a designated test set, this means: (a) hold out a random 5K WavCaps audio-caption pairs from training, (b) retrain all Stage 45 conditions without these held-out pairs, (c) evaluate av_at on the WavCaps holdout. If av_at on WavCaps holdout for WavCaps-trained models is comparable to Stage 44's av_at on AudioCaps (≈0.22), then caption quality is not the issue — the WavCaps model is learning WavCaps audio-text alignment fine, and the gap is entirely distribution shift. If av_at on WavCaps holdout is still low (≈0.11), then quality genuinely limits Phase B bridge formation even in-distribution, strongly supporting the quality claim. This experiment requires retraining all Stage 45 conditions (4 conditions × 5 seeds × 3 dims = 60 training runs). Effort: approximately 1 week.

An intermediate option: evaluate the existing Stage 45 WavCaps-trained checkpoints on a different audio-text benchmark that is more acoustically similar to WavCaps (e.g., Clotho, which uses Freesound audio — the same source as WavCaps/Freesound). This avoids retraining and costs only evaluation time. If WavCaps-trained models achieve higher av_at on Clotho than on AudioCaps, distribution shift is confirmed as a significant factor.

Decision: The experimental fix is worth doing, and the Clotho intermediate option makes it accessible.

This is the highest scientific-stakes experiment in the list. If the quality-vs-shift question is left unanswered, a reviewer can correctly argue that the paper's central finding ("data quality, not scale") is confounded. The written fix (acknowledging the confound) is necessary but insufficient on its own for a top venue. The Clotho evaluation intermediate option is low cost (hours, not days) and provides direct evidence. If the results support quality as a driver (WavCaps models underperform even on Clotho despite matching acoustic distribution), the paper's claim is substantially strengthened. If distribution shift dominates, the paper needs to be rewritten — but that rewrite would be grounded in a correct understanding of the system. Either way, running the experiment is the right scientific decision. This and W2 are the two experiments most worth doing.

W4 — Weak theoretical motivation for the geometric mean form
The scientific issue: Both frameworks motivating the geometric mean form have gaps. Framework A (symmetry argument) uses phase-order symmetry to derive a = b, but symmetry holds only approximately and only when both phases use comparable data and methods. The argument from first-order homogeneity is an explicit modeling assumption, not derived from the experimental setup. Framework B (Cauchy-Schwarz factorization) requires z_i ≈ W_i z_t and z_a ≈ W_a z_t — a text-mediated factorization assumption. The quality of this approximation is never measured.

The key insight: Stage 53 data already exists. Stage 53 (SDPI constrained channel analysis) computes R²_it from a Ridge regression of zt → zi and R²_at from zt → za. These R² values directly measure how well text embeddings predict image and audio embeddings — which is exactly the quality of the Cauchy-Schwarz factorization approximation. From the SDPI results: corr(eta_geom, alpha_recall) = +0.689 where eta_geom = sqrt(R²_it × R²_at). This means the factorization quality is correlated with the transmission efficiency α, which is exactly what Framework B predicts: methods with better factorization (embeddings better predicted by text) have higher α.

The experimental fix in practice: Pull the R² values from Stage 53 for each condition. Report the mean R²_it (how well text predicts image embeddings) as a measurement of Cauchy-Schwarz approximation quality. If R²_it ≈ 0.35–0.45, the approximation explains roughly one-third to half of image embedding variance, which is moderate. This is a data extraction and reporting task, not new experimentation. Cost: hours.

The written fix in practice: Rewrite the theory section header from "Statement and Fit" to "Motivating Frameworks and Empirical Fit." Add one sentence to Framework B: "We measure the quality of the factorization approximation via Ridge regression of text embeddings onto image embeddings (R²_it; Stage 53 analysis): at m=256, R²_it ≈ [value] for modular_shared_jl, indicating the factorization captures roughly [X]% of image embedding variance. Methods with tighter factorization (higher R²_it × R²_at) achieve higher transmission efficiency α (r=+0.689 across conditions), consistent with the Cauchy-Schwarz ceiling interpretation." Add to the limitations: "Neither framework constitutes a proof that the geometric mean form must hold exactly. Framework A requires the symmetry assumption to hold universally across design choices; Framework B requires the factorization approximation to be reasonably tight. The r²=0.823 empirical fit confirms the law's practical predictive value under the conditions tested."

Decision: Written fix with Stage 53 data is almost free and substantially improves the theory section.

This is the best example of an "existing data" fix. The Stage 53 analysis was already run. Reporting the R²_it measurement in the paper costs essentially nothing and converts a vague claim ("the factorization is approximate") into a quantified statement ("the factorization explains ≈35% of variance"). The written framing change (motivating framework vs. derivation) is also necessary and costs only careful editing. Together, these make the theory section honest without abandoning the structural motivation. No new experiments are needed.

W5 — Out-of-sample test validates interpolation, not extrapolation
The scientific issue: The out-of-sample test fits α on AudioCaps observations (n=300) and predicts WavCaps observations (n=80), obtaining r=0.953. This tests: does the geometric mean form extrapolate correctly when av_at changes (WavCaps lowers av_at) for the same methods? The answer is yes, and it is not surprising — the geometric mean form was derived partly by observing the WavCaps scaling results. A stronger test would be: given a new projection method's geometry (centroid gap, measured without retrieval evaluation), can you predict its α before running retrieval? That would demonstrate α is a measurable property of the embedding space, not a post-hoc curve-fitting parameter.

Stage 39 data already exists: Stage 39 measures Gap_ia (L2 centroid gap between image and audio embeddings) for each method at each dimension. The per-method α values are in Table 4. A linear regression from Gap_ia to α uses entirely pre-existing data. If this regression is tight (r²>0.7), it establishes that α can be read off the embedding geometry — meaning the law has genuine predictive content for new methods, not just for held-out data conditions from the same methods.

The experimental fix in practice: Compute the Gap_ia → α linear regression using Stage 39's centroid gap measurements and Table 4's per-method α values. This is a simple R or Python computation on existing data. Cost: 30 minutes. Report the regression R² and the fit. This transforms the out-of-sample claim from "the law extrapolates to held-out data conditions" to "α can be predicted from a geometric measurement before retrieval evaluation."

The written fix in practice: Add one paragraph after the current out-of-sample test: "We additionally note that α is not purely a post-hoc parameter: the centroid gap (Gap_ia; Appendix D) predicts per-method α with r²=X (linear regression across N method-dimension observations; Stage 39 data). This indicates that α reflects a measurable property of the projection geometry, and a new method's α can be estimated from its embedding geometry before running retrieval evaluation. Together with the held-out predictive test, this supports the bottleneck law as a predictive tool rather than a descriptive fit."

Decision: This is a nearly free experiment (30-minute computation on existing data) that substantially strengthens the law's most important claim.

The current out-of-sample test is the paper's primary defense against "this is just curve-fitting." Replacing it with a geometry-to-α prediction is much stronger: it shows that α is an independently measurable quantity, which makes the law genuinely predictive in the sense that matters. The data exists; the analysis takes minutes. This should definitely be done.

W6 — CLAP alignment margin circularity
The scientific issue: The recommended pre-training filter (CLAP margin > 0.5) is circular when the Phase B encoder is CLAP: AudioCaps scores high on the CLAP margin partly because AudioCaps is in CLAP's training distribution. The recommendation would mislead a practitioner applying the protocol to a different audio-text dataset where CLAP may have been trained on similar data. For a genuinely new domain (medical audio, industrial sounds), the CLAP margin may systematically mislabel high-quality captions as low-quality because the CLAP model doesn't generalize.

The experimental fix in practice: Compute a text-only specificity score (e.g., embed captions with a frozen BERT or sentence transformer and measure average intra-set pairwise distance — more specific captions are more distant from each other in sentence embedding space) and show it correlates with the CLAP margin across audio-caption sources. Cost: one afternoon. However, this only shows that text-only measures can approximate the CLAP margin, not that the text-only measure predicts downstream Phase B performance — which would require retraining Phase B with filtered vs unfiltered data by the text-only criterion. Full experimental validation of the text-only filter is a week of work.

The written fix in practice: Add one sentence to Section 5.2 or the quality appendix: "The CLAP alignment margin is a practical proxy under conditions where the quality-assessment model (CLAP) is not strongly biased toward or against the evaluation distribution. For AudioCaps specifically, CLAP is trained on similar data and may score it favorably independent of caption quality; for truly out-of-distribution audio domains, a text-only specificity measure (e.g., caption distinctiveness under a frozen language model) may be preferable as it avoids encoder-specific biases. We use the CLAP margin as the primary diagnostic given its strong correlation with observed retrieval quality in our setting." This is two sentences added to an existing paragraph.

Decision: Written fix is sufficient.

The circularity is real but bounded. Stage 38's CLAP margin analysis is evidence in the specific system being studied (CLAP features + AudioCaps evaluation), and the correlation between margin and retrieval performance is empirically valid within that system. The recommendation ("filter to margin > 0.5") is sound for the described setup. Extending it to a general prescription requires caveating the encoder dependency, which a written fix accomplishes. The experimental validation of a text-only alternative would be genuinely valuable for a journal version of this paper but is not necessary for NeurIPS acceptance.

W7 — No quantitative comparison to prior work
The scientific issue: The paper cites AudioCLIP and LanguageBind as related work but doesn't compare against either quantitatively. AudioCLIP directly adds audio to CLIP via joint fine-tuning on audio-image-text triplets — this is the most directly comparable prior method. Without knowing how AudioCLIP performs on the same evaluation pool, readers cannot assess whether the proposed protocol is competitive, complementary, or clearly inferior to the existing state of the art. The paper's framing ("we understand what determines quality") is stronger if paired with "and our best method is within X% of a system that uses direct supervision."

The experimental fix in practice: Load the publicly available AudioCLIP checkpoint. Run inference on the AudioCaps 4,411-item test pool using the same av_ia and av_at metrics. This is one inference run — no training. Cost: 2–3 hours including setup. AudioCLIP's checkpoint is available at its GitHub repository. LanguageBind's checkpoint is also available and similarly evaluable. The resulting comparison table would anchor the paper's absolute performance numbers in the landscape of prior work.

The written fix in practice: Look up published av_ia or audio-image retrieval numbers from AudioCLIP and LanguageBind's papers. Note the evaluation protocol differences (they use a different test set, or a different gallery size). Add a footnote to Table 1: "AudioCLIP reports R@1 of X on the AudioCaps 1K test subset using a CLIP audio head trained jointly with audio-image-text data; direct comparison is confounded by evaluation protocol and backbone differences." This is better than nothing, but a reviewer will note that the comparison is imprecise and push for running the comparison on a common evaluation set.

Decision: The experimental fix is worth doing and is genuinely low-cost.

Running AudioCLIP inference on the existing test set takes hours and provides a direct, protocol-matched comparison. This is one of the lowest-effort, highest-reviewer-satisfaction fixes in the list. Reviewers at NeurIPS consistently penalize the absence of comparison to directly related prior work, especially when those methods are publicly available. The practical cost is very low relative to the presentation benefit. If AudioCLIP performs comparably to the proposed protocol despite using direct image-audio supervision, that is a striking result (modular text-anchored training matches AudioCLIP's direct supervision). If AudioCLIP performs much better, the paper should acknowledge the gap honestly and note that the contribution is the analytical framework and bottleneck law, not state-of-the-art performance.

W8 — Non-standard evaluation protocol (4,411 vs 1K test pool)
The scientific issue: Most prior work on AudioCaps retrieval evaluates on a 1K test subset (1,000 audio clips × 5 captions = 5K retrieval targets in one direction). The paper uses the full 4,411-item test pool, which makes the retrieval task approximately 4.4× harder (larger gallery → lower recall). This means all reported numbers in the paper are systematically lower than published numbers from CLAP, AudioCLIP, and other baselines — not because the method is worse, but because the evaluation is harder. A reader cannot assess whether av_at=0.225 for the linear probe is competitive with CLAP's reported audio-text retrieval numbers without knowing the evaluation protocol translation.

The experimental fix in practice: Subset the existing test pool to the standard 1K split. This requires identifying which 1,000 of the 4,411 test items belong to the standard subset — this information is in the AudioCaps dataset documentation. Once identified, run the existing trained checkpoints through the same evaluation function with the 1K gallery. No retraining. Cost: 1–2 hours. Report both sets of numbers — full 4,411 pool in all main tables (for internal consistency across ablations) and the 1K subset numbers in Appendix or Table 1 footnote for comparability with prior work.

The written fix in practice: Add to Section 3 (Evaluation Metrics): "We use the full 4,411-item AudioCaps test pool for all experiments in this paper to maximize coverage and maintain internal consistency across ablations. This pool is approximately 4.4× larger than the 1K test subset used in some prior work; recall numbers are proportionally lower and not directly comparable to published results from CLAP or AudioCLIP. Appendix X reports numbers on the standard 1K split for direct comparison." Even without running the experiment, explicitly flagging the protocol difference prevents a reviewer from mistaking "low absolute numbers" for "poor performance."

Decision: This experiment should definitely be done. It is trivially easy.

This is the highest return-to-effort ratio fix in the entire list. Subsetting the existing test set and re-running evaluation costs 1–2 hours of engineering time. Without this, every reviewer who knows AudioCaps will wonder why the paper's av_at=0.225 looks different from CLAP's reported numbers. The "we used a different eval split" explanation can be given in writing, but providing actual numbers on the standard split removes any ambiguity. This should be done before submission regardless of other priorities.

W9 — Sharing-factorial reversal at m=256 is fragile
The scientific issue: The Phase A sharing reversal (helps at m=64,128; hurts at m=256; neutral at m=512) is a novel finding presented with statistical significance (p=0.005 Holm-corrected within the m=256 condition). However, the significance is tested per-dimension; there is no correction across all four dimension-effect combinations simultaneously. If Bonferroni correction is applied across 8 tests (4 dimensions × 2 sharing levels), the threshold for significance drops to 0.05/8 = 0.00625. The m=256 Phase A effect at p=0.005 still survives this correction, but only barely. More importantly, a sign reversal at one of four tested values of m is inherently less convincing than a monotonic effect — the reversal requires a specific mechanistic story ("at m=256 the shared projection actively conflicts with Phase B's needs") that is not independently verified.

The experimental fix in practice: Run 5 new training seeds at m=256 for the sharing-factorial conditions (Phase A shared/separate × Phase B shared/separate). This requires 4 conditions × 5 new seeds = 20 additional training runs, all at m=256 only. Cost: 1–2 days. If the reversal replicates (shared Phase A still significantly hurts at m=256 with the new seeds), the finding is substantially more credible. If the reversal disappears or weakens, the paper should downgrade the claim from "significant finding" to "trend warranting further investigation."

The written fix in practice: Add to Table 3 footnote: "Significance values are Holm-corrected within each embedding dimension; applying joint correction across all 8 dimension-effect combinations (Bonferroni threshold 0.00625) does not change which effects survive (m=256 Phase A reversal remains p=0.005). This finding is based on 5 seeds and has not been independently replicated; we report it as a direction-reversal warranting mechanistic investigation rather than a fully established result." This is honest, acknowledges the fragility, and preempts the reviewer's concern without abandoning the finding.

Decision: Written fix is sufficient, but the experimental replication is worth doing if 1–2 days are available.

The written fix (joint correction, explicit fragility caveat) is sufficient for reviewer satisfaction — the p=0.005 effect does survive even stricter correction, and a 5-seed study is a reasonable sample for an ablation finding. The experimental replication would transform the finding from "intriguing" to "established." Given that the reversal is the most novel mechanistic claim in the sharing section (the direction of sharing effects is predicted by theory for monotone cases; a reversal is not), replicating it has scientific value. If the replication budget is limited to 1 item, this is lower priority than W2, W3, and W7.

W10 — Privacy/federated appendix is incongruent
The scientific issue: The appendix reports (a) neural feature-inversion error for JLT+Mahalanobis on image-text COCO retrieval (Phase A, not audio), and (b) FedAvg results for the Phase A image-text model. Neither is connected to the paper's stated contribution (modular multimodal extension, bottleneck law, audio transitivity). The federated result is for Phase A alone; Phase B federated training is not described or evaluated. The privacy result is for JLT specifically, the worst-performing projection in all ablations. A reviewer reaching this appendix will conclude either that the paper was repurposed from a prior JLT-focused submission or that the authors are trying to inflate the apparent scope with unrelated material.

The experimental fix in practice: None needed.

The written fix in practice: Remove the appendix. If there is a genuine privacy angle to the modular protocol — specifically, that Phase B training never requires image data on the audio-training machine, making the protocol inherently data-silo-compatible — that claim should be a 2-sentence observation in the conclusion or limitations section, not an appendix with experimental tables. The tables in the current appendix (neural inversion error, federated gap numbers) add no value to the paper's contribution and dilute focus.

Decision: Remove the appendix. This is a pure writing decision.

There is no scientific upside to keeping this appendix in its current form. The cost of removal is zero (delete two pages). The benefit is a more focused paper with no vestigial content for reviewers to question.

W11 — Identity ablation (Stage 47) result is arguably expected
The scientific issue: The paper claims Stage 47 demonstrates "Phase B is the sole mechanism for audio alignment." A skeptical reviewer will note that this is trivially true for any gradient-based system: a randomly initialized linear layer, never updated, produces random outputs. The ablation would be non-trivial if there were a plausible alternative mechanism — for example, if CLAP and CLIP shared a significant portion of their training data or encoder architecture, creating systematic pre-alignment. Without establishing that pre-alignment was a live hypothesis, the ablation tests a strawman.

The experimental fix in practice: The non-trivial version of this ablation is training Phase B with randomly shuffled audio-caption pairs — same number of gradient steps, same architecture, same InfoNCE loss, but audio is paired with random captions instead of its own. This tests whether any gradient signal (even semantically incorrect signal) produces above-chance audio alignment. If yes, the architecture itself imposes structure independent of semantic content. If no (shuffled captions → chance performance), Phase B requires not just gradient updates but semantically valid audio-text signal. The distinction between "no Phase B" and "noisy Phase B" is scientifically meaningful. Cost: 5 additional training runs (1 condition, 5 seeds). Cost: 4–6 hours.

The written fix in practice: Reframe Stage 47 as a necessary-condition verification rather than a discovery. "We verify that Phase B training is a necessary condition for above-chance audio alignment — consistent with the expected behavior of a gradient-based system starting from random initialization. The Stage 47 identity ablation establishes that above-chance av_ia in Table 1 is not attributable to CLAP-CLIP architectural overlap or shared pretraining data creating background alignment. The 30× performance gap between identity ablation and trained modular_shared_jl (av_ia=0.034 vs 0.0015) is entirely a Phase B InfoNCE learning effect." This reframe is accurate and doesn't oversell the result.

Decision: Written fix is sufficient. The shuffled-captions experiment is a scientific upgrade but not necessary.

The identity ablation is a valid methodological control. Its value is confirming that the observed transitivity is not an artifact of backbone pre-alignment — which is a reasonable thing to verify, even if the alternative hypothesis is unlikely. The written reframe (necessary condition verification, not surprising discovery) makes the claim appropriate in scope. The shuffled-captions experiment would add genuine scientific value (testing whether any gradient signal helps) but is not required to defend the current result at a NeurIPS reviewer level. If the shuffled-captions run can be done quickly (it can — 5 training runs), it is a nice upgrade; if time is limited, skip it.

W12 — JLT-heavy structure is inconsistent with the stated narrative
The scientific issue: The paper states in Section 1 that "JLT is one of several projection design choices evaluated, not the hero." But JLT dominates the experimental real estate: Table 1 leads with modular_shared_jl, the projection-sharing factorial (Section 5.3) uses only JLT variants, the bottleneck law per-method table has four JLT rows and two non-JLT rows, and the appendix's privacy and centroid gap results are JLT-specific. The linear probe — which is the best-performing method and the one the paper recommends — gets one subsection and one table column. A reviewer will notice the mismatch between stated focus and actual content.

The experimental fix in practice: None needed.

The written fix in practice: Two structural changes. First, reorder Table 1 to lead with audio_linear_probe followed by modular_shared_jl as the secondary method. This is cosmetic but signals to a reviewer that the linear probe is the primary result. Second, add a sentence to Section 5.3 (Projection Sharing): "The sharing factorial uses JLT variants exclusively; whether the dimension-dependent reversal extends to linear probe sharing is not investigated here and is a natural direction for follow-up work." This acknowledges the JLT-centricity of the sharing result without requiring new experiments. Third, in the per-method bottleneck law table, group the JLT variants in a footnote or collapsed row ("JLT variants (4 methods): α=0.244–0.273") and give the non-JLT methods (linear probe, LoRA proxy) their own rows.

Decision: Pure writing fix. Restructure tables and add one sentence. No new experiments.

This is a presentation and framing problem, not a scientific problem. The JLT experiments are all valid and the results are correctly reported. The fix is reordering and re-emphasizing. It costs editing time only.

W13 — audio_R metric is undefined in the main paper
The scientific issue: The metric audio_R = (av_at + av_ia) / 2 appears in the dimension-conditional tradeoff paragraph of the LaTeX paper without prior definition. Reviewers seeing this notation for the first time will be confused. More substantively, the 50/50 weighting is not motivated. The paper's primary evaluation target is av_ia (zero-shot image-audio transfer); av_at is a bridge quality measure. Weighting them equally implies that audio-text bridge quality is as important as image-audio transitivity, which conflicts with the paper's stated focus.

The experimental fix in practice: None needed.

The written fix: Two options. Option A: Define audio_R formally in Section 3 as "audio_R = (av_at + av_ia)/2 — a composite metric used when Stage A checkpoints are shared across conditions, making coco_avg_R identical and audio_R the only source of variation between conditions." This justifies the metric in the specific context where it appears (Stage 45 condition comparison). Option B: Drop audio_R from the paper entirely and report av_at and av_ia in separate columns throughout, which is more informative and uses standard notation. Option B is cleaner; the dimension-conditional tradeoff paragraph already reports av_at and av_ia separately, so audio_R is redundant there.

Decision: Drop audio_R from the LaTeX paper and report av_at and av_ia separately.

The metric was useful for internal analysis (comparing Stage 45 conditions where coco_avg_R is frozen), but in the paper it just adds notation. Every table that reports av_at and av_ia separately is already giving more information than audio_R. Remove it from the LaTeX paper; keep it in revised_narrative.md for internal comparisons.

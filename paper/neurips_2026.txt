Abstract
Multimodal systems are usually built by jointly training on paired data for every
modality combination, which makes them prohibitively expensive to expand—
adding a new modality typically requires retraining the entire system from scratch.
This paper shows a modular alternative: start from a frozen image–text model,
align audio to the same text space in a separate training stage, and obtain zero-shot
image–audio retrieval without ever using image–audio pairs. The approach works
strikingly well, achieving retrieval above 30× chance on a standard benchmark for
strictly modular projection-only methods (Linear Probe and JL variants), despite
never seeing an image paired with a sound.
Causal ablations establish that the transfer is not an artifact of pre-trained backbone
similarity: removing the audio-text training stage entirely collapses retrieval to
chance across all tested embedding dimensions, and training on semantically
scrambled captions collapses it equally. We derive a bottleneck theory showing that
performance depends multiplicatively on both text-anchored alignment strengths,
scaled by an encoder-domain efficiency factor that captures how well the audio
encoder’s training distribution matches the target domain. This structure predicts
held-out WavCaps performance with high accuracy and, across three audio domains,
correctly attributes near-complete failure in a speech setting to encoder mismatch
rather than protocol breakdown.

Introduction

Pretrained bimodal models such as CLIP [Radford et al., 2021] and ALIGN [Jia et al., 2021] build
rich shared embedding spaces for image and text. A natural follow-on question is whether a new
perceptual modality—audio, depth, video, thermal—can be added to this space without retraining the
existing components and without collecting direct cross-modal supervision between the new modality
and the existing ones.
The text-anchored protocol. We study a text-anchored modular construction: first, an image-text
alignment stage trains projection heads on COCO image-caption pairs and freezes all components
after convergence. Separately, an audio-text alignment stage trains a new audio projection head on
AudioCaps, using the frozen text head as a semantic bridge—both image and audio embeddings
are pulled toward the same text anchor, so zero-shot image-audio retrieval emerges at inference
without image-audio training pairs. The audio-text stage is modular in the strict sense: it can run
independently on any machine that holds only audio-text data and never modifies the image-text
components. We refer to the image-text and audio-text training stages as Phase A and Phase B
respectively throughout the paper.

ImageBind and the anchoring choice. ImageBind [Girdhar et al., 2023] shows that using images
as a common anchor trains cross-modal transitivity across six modalities simultaneously with direct
supervision. Our protocol differs in three ways: (1) the anchor is text, not images; (2) Phases A and
B are decoupled—no joint retraining; (3) Phase B requires no image data at all. These constraints
make the protocol substantially easier to deploy in data-limited settings, but raise the question: what
determines the quality of transitivity that emerges?

Related Work

Joint cross-modal training. Joint multimodal systems train all modality pairs simultaneously with
direct supervision. AudioCLIP [Guzhov et al., 2022] extends CLIP by fine-tuning on image-audiotext triplets, requiring concurrent access to all three modalities; on our 1K evaluation split it achieves
Ria = 0.335 (Appendix P). ImageBind [Girdhar et al., 2023] anchors six modalities to images with
direct supervision and a ViT-H/14 backbone; its Ria = 0.257 on our full pool reflects both backbone
scale and direct image-audio training. Our protocol differs in kind: it adds audio using only audio-text
data, without modifying the existing image-text model and without any image-audio pairs.
Text as a cross-modal anchor. Using text as a universal semantic bridge is implicit in large
vision-language models such as ALIGN [Jia et al., 2021] and BLIP [Li et al., 2022], which show
that rich language representations generalise across modality boundaries. LanguageBind [Zhu et al.,
2023] explicitly aligns video, depth, audio, and thermal modalities to a shared language space, but
does so jointly with direct supervision across all pairs rather than incrementally. Our contribution
is to isolate and characterise the zero-shot transitivity that emerges when two separately trained
language-anchored alignments are composed at inference time, and to derive a quantitative theory
governing its quality.
Modular and continual multimodal learning. Parameter-efficient adapters such as VL-Adapter
[Sung et al., 2022] extend pretrained models to new vision-language tasks without full retraining,
but do not address the case where a new perceptual modality must be added with no cross-modal
paired data. In the audio domain, CLAP [Elizalde et al., 2022] demonstrates strong audio-language
alignment from frozen pretrained audio encoders, which we adopt as our Phase B backbone. Our
work is distinctive in (a) strict modularity—Phase B runs on any machine with only audio-text data
and never touches Phase A components—and (b) systematic derivation of a quantitative predictive
theory rather than architectural proposals.
Random projection and geometric alignment. Johnson-Lindenstrauss projections [Johnson &
Lindenstrauss, 1984] and sparser variants [Kane & Nelson, 2014] provide dimension-reduction
guarantees with no learned parameters. Prior work has used JL-style projections for cross-modal
embedding alignment [Garg et al., 2021]. Our contribution to this line is to show that the centroid

Protocol and Experimental Setup

Two-phase text-anchored training. Phase A trains a projection head fv : Rdv → Rm for image
features and ft : Rdt → Rm for text features using symmetric InfoNCE on COCO image-text pairs.
After convergence, both heads are frozen. Phase B trains a projection head fa : Rda → Rm for audio
features using symmetric InfoNCE on AudioCaps audio-text pairs with the frozen ft as the text
encoder. Zero-shot image-audio retrieval at test time uses fv and fa without any further adaptation.
Projection designs. We evaluate four Phase B projection designs: (i) Linear probe: a single
learnable matrix W ∈ Rm×da trained on Phase B; (ii) JL variants: a fixed Kane-Nelson sparse
random matrix Φ ∈ Rm×da [Kane & Nelson, 2014] followed by a learnable Mahalanobis head
(shared or separate from Phase A, hybrid-AT or hybrid-IT); (iii) LoRA adapter (text-head proxy): a
low-rank adapter that initialises its weights from the frozen text projection head and adapts them to
the audio domain via Phase B training—we call it a “proxy” because it uses the text head’s parameters
as a structured geometric initialisation rather than a random one; (iv) Joint references: clip-head or
JL trained with image-audio-text data jointly (higher-supervision reference rows).
Datasets and features. Phase A: COCO 2017, 118K training images, 5 captions each. Phase B:
AudioCaps [Kim et al., 2019], ∼46K audio-caption pairs (training split). Phase B scaling: WavCaps
[Mei et al., 2023], ∼400K audio-caption pairs. We extract frozen backbone features: CLIP ViT-B/32
for images and text (dv = 768, dt = 512); CLAP [Elizalde et al., 2022] for audio (da = 512).
Evaluation metrics. Ria denotes mean recall at 1, 5, 10 for image→audio and audio→image
on the AudioCaps test split (4,411 examples; Rchance = 0.0011). Rat denotes mean recall at 1,
5, 10 for audio→text on the same split. Rit denotes image-text recall on the same split (evaluates
the Phase A bridge quality OOD). P @1cat denotes category-level precision at 1 averaged over 20
coarse acoustic categories (chance = 0.05). We report two semantic-label protocols: CLAP-label
is the primary full-coverage semantic metric, while metadata-grounded labeling is an independent
robustness check on the explicitly keyword-labeled subset (Appendix B). To ground R in standard
metrics: at m = 512, the top modular methods reach R@1 = 0.007, R@5 = 0.033, R@10 = 0.057
(full tables in Appendix F, Table 10). All results are averaged over 5 training seeds unless noted; we
report mean ± std. Unless noted, all retrieval metrics are evaluated on the full 4,411-item AudioCaps
test pool. A 1K split (883 items; one clip per unique YouTube ID, first occurrence in the test
split) is used to remove the multi-caption inflation artifact in item-count statistics and enables direct
comparison with external baselines reported on a single-clip-per-video criterion; see Appendix P.
Reproducibility. All results average over 5 independent training seeds; mean ± std is reported
throughout. Pre-computed backbone features (CLIP ViT-B/32; CLAP HTSAT) are fixed across
all projection-head training conditions, so the only randomness across seeds is projection-head
initialization and data-loader shuffling. Each Stage identifier in the text corresponds to a reproducible

Transitivity Emerges from Text Anchoring

Table 1 shows the core result. The audio linear probe achieves Ria = 0.0352 ± 0.006 at m = 512—
32× above chance—and the modular shared-JL model achieves Ria = 0.0339 ± 0.001—31×
above chance—despite neither model having observed an image-audio pair during training. This
comparison is not evidence of modular-training superiority: the original joint rows are encoderconfounded (CLAP vs. CLIP-style audio heads). The encoder-matched W2 row (Joint CLAP Head,
5 new seeds) gives Ria = 0.0414 at m = 512, and the Linear Probe reaches 85% of that reference
(0.0352 vs. 0.0414) without image-audio supervision.
Evidence preview. Three key findings that underpin and contextualise the headline result are
developed in later sections; we preview them here for orientation. Causal necessity (Figure 2;
details in Appendix D): skipping the audio-text training stage (Stage 47; identity ablation: Phase B
training skipped, audio head at random initialisation) collapses Ria to chance at all embedding
dimensions; a shuffled-caption control (W11/W12; audio-caption assignments randomised while
keeping the full training loop) produces the same collapse, ruling out backbone
pre-alignment as a
p
confound. Predictive theory (Section 6): the transitivity bottleneck Ria ≈ α Rit · Rat fits in-sample
at r = 0.921 (n = 300) and predicts held-out WavCaps conditions at r = 0.953. Cross-domain range
(Appendix L, M): the transmission efficiency α varies from 0.020 (SpeechCoco, near-chance transfer
despite functional bridge legs) to 0.673 (AVCaps), spanning more than an order of magnitude under
identical encoder and protocol. Additional diagnostics (joint-α decomposition, 1K-split comparisons,
category-level semantic transfer, dimension sweep) are in Appendix N, P, A, B, and C.
The LoRA anomaly. The LoRA Proxy achieves the highest modular Ria at m = 512 (0.0389)
yet the weakest theory fit (r = 0.293, Table 5). This apparent paradox is resolved by its design:
because the LoRA Proxy initialises from the frozen text head’s weights, it begins in a well-aligned
subspace and can exploit residual structure in the text head’s geometry—a retrieval advantage that is
not predictable from Rit and Rat alone, precisely because this initialisation breaks the independence
assumption underlying the bottleneck law. The LoRA Proxy is best understood as a hybrid: modular
in that it trains only on audio-text data, but carrying structural information from the image-text stage

Under COCO Phase A, the linear probe and JL model achieve comparable image-audio retrieval
(0.0352 vs. 0.0339, 4% difference). The LP’s higher mean comes with substantially greater variance
(±.006 vs. ±.001), making this 4% difference statistically non-significant (Welch’s t ≈ 0.48, p ≈

appeared in earlier results because the linear probe used CC3M Phase A (3M pairs) while the JL
model used COCO Phase A (118K pairs); the richer CC3M pre-training provided a stronger text
bridge specifically for the unconstrained linear probe. This apparent tension with global α values is
resolved by dimension-conditional fitting: global αLP /αJL = 1.386 predicts an LP/JL ratio of 1.471
at m = 512, but the observed ratio is 1.037; fitting α at fixed dimension gives αLP /αJL = 0.979
at m = 512, predicting 1.038 (Appendix O, Table 20). Global α conflates cross-dimension and
cross-method variance; at fixed m = 512, the near-equal centroid gaps (0.506 vs. 0.533) correctly
imply near-equal efficiency. Notably, the conditional fit reverses the LP/JL ordering at m = 512
(αJL = 0.320 > αLP = 0.313), consistent with the centroid gaps differing by only 5% at that
dimension; LP’s global advantage arises entirely from larger centroid-gap differences at smaller m.

Centroid gap predicts transmission efficiency. The centroid gap is not merely a post-hoc geometric description: it quantitatively predicts α. Regressing centroid gap (Gapia ) against α across
all (method, dimension, seed) triples yields a pooled Pearson r = −0.805 (p < 0.001, n = 40):
larger centroid gaps correlate with lower transmission efficiency. The relationship is stronger within
each embedding dimension: r = −0.982 at m = 64, r = −0.996 at m = 128, r = −0.938 at
m = 256, and r = −0.773 at m = 512, confirming that the centroid gap is a mechanistic driver of
α rather than a co-variate. Conversely, α predicts Ria : r = +0.766 across the same sample. These
regressions support a mechanistic pathway consistent with Phase B projection geometry (centroid gap)
→ transmission efficiency (α) → zero-shot retrieval (Ria ), providing a predictive handle on retrieval
performance from geometric inspection. We also ran a direct intervention (Stage 67; centroid-gap
regularisation added during Phase B training): adding centroid-gap regularization during Phase B
increases Ria by +0.0032 at m = 256 (Holm-adjusted p = 0.0115) and +0.0039 at m = 512
(Holm-adjusted p = 0.0256), with only a small non-significant Rat decrease. This supports a causal
interpretation of centroid geometry as a controllable post-training lever. By contrast, a pre-Phase-B
feasibility check (Stage 68-D; pre-training centroid gap measured before Phase B begins) shows that
initialization-time centroid gap has weak predictive value for final α (r2 = 0.046), so geometry is
better used as a post-training diagnostic than a pre-flight predictor. Both Gapia values (Stage 39) and
α values (Stage 36) use COCO Phase A checkpoints throughout.

WavCaps holdout retrain separates quality from split leakage. To separate caption quality effects
from evaluation leakage, we reran WavCaps Phase B with an explicit in-source holdout (Stage 56;
Appendix K). On AudioCaps evaluation, all WavCaps variants remain far below AudioCaps-trained
Phase B (Rat ∈ [0.093, 0.111] vs. 0.226), confirming the quality bottleneck. On held-out WavCaps
evaluation, source composition becomes decisive: clean-source variants are near parity or slightly
better than their AudioCaps-eval scores (∆holdout-at = +0.001 to +0.003), while mixed-source
variants drop sharply (−0.021 to −0.031), indicating that noisy mixed sources drive most of the
cross-domain degradation. Thus the robust conclusion is quality-first with source-match: more data
helps only when the added captions preserve alignment quality and source consistency. Within the
WavCaps variants themselves, however, scale still contributes: the 200K mixed-source condition
outperforms the clean-source condition on AudioCaps evaluation (Ria = 0.028 vs. 0.024), indicating
that quality and scale jointly govern performance once source distribution is held fixed.
Dimension-conditional data-scale tradeoff. The analysis above uses m = 512. The
Stage 56 W3 extension (all four WavCaps conditions, 5 seeds each, m ∈ {64, 128, 256, 512})
confirms that the data-scale effect on Ria is dimension-conditional.
Comparing 200K
vs. 46K WavCaps mixed-source conditions: Rat improves at every dimension (∆ =
+0.0135, +0.0122, +0.0124, +0.0089 from m = 64 → 512), while Ria follows a crossover pattern
(∆ = −0.0004, +0.0028, −0.0001, −0.0005). Thus larger WavCaps scale reliably strengthens the
audio-text bridge but does not reliably translate to better image-audio transitivity once dimension is
high. We interpret this as a capacity-enabled specialization effect: at larger m, additional capacity can
fit WavCaps audio-text semantics without preserving alignment to the COCO-anchored image space.
The practical recommendation is therefore: if WavCaps-scale data must be used, prefer m ≤ 256
where transitivity is stable. Across all dimensions, AudioCaps at 46K captions outperforms all WavCaps conditions by about a factor of 2 on both Rat and Ria (mean across m ∈ {64, 128, 256, 512}, 5
seeds each), confirming that caption quality and source-match remain the primary levers over raw data
scale. Adding the fifth WavCaps condition (100K mixed-source, W13) reinforces this interpretation
at m = 512: within-domain audio-text quality improves smoothly with scale, but cross-domain
image-audio transfer remains non-monotonic across 46K, 100K, and 200K mixed-source settings.

Theoretical motivation. The geometric-mean form above is motivated by the independence of
the two bridge traversals. If image and audio embeddings each reach the shared text space through
statistically independent errors—that is, the residual misalignment of the image head and the residual
misalignment of the audio head are uncorrelated in the Rm text space—then expected cross-modal
overlap scales as the product of the individual bridge reliabilities. Taking the square root gives
the geometric mean of the two recall values. The α factor absorbs remaining correlation structure
(encoder architecture, training-distribution overlap) not captured by marginal bridge qualities alone;
its near-constancy within a modality triple (r2 = 0.848, n = 300) supports this approximation, while
its variation across triples (α ∈ [0.020, 0.673]) quantifies where the approximation weakens.
Table 5 shows that Eq. 1 captures most cross-condition variance in the primary suite (r = 0.921,
r2 = 0.848, n = 300; MAE = 0.00415). The 300 observations span approximately 60 independent
(method, embedding dimension, data-source) condition cells at 5 seeds each; within-cell seed variance
is small relative to cross-condition variance, so the seed-level r = 0.921 is a conservative estimate—
cell-mean r ≥ 0.921, since averaging 5 seeds per cell reduces within-cell noise without discarding
cross-condition variance. The theory is strong across experimental conditions (varying method, data
source, or embedding dimension) and weak by design within any fixed condition where Rit has
near-zero variance across seeds; the out-of-sample test below provides the cleanest independence
check.

we fit α only on AudioCaps-trained observations (n = 220; all sources except WavCaps scaling) and
predict held-out WavCaps observations (n = 80). The held-out fit remains stable (α = 0.282), with

Limitations

Absolute performance gap. At best, Ria = 0.039 on the 4,411-item AudioCaps test pool (LoRA
Proxy, m = 512), or 0.129 on the 883-item 1K split (Linear Probe). This is well above chance but far

Conclusion

We have characterized zero-shot cross-modal transitivity in text-anchored modular multimodal learning through controlled experiments and a predictive quantitative theory. The protocol is effective:
modular text-anchored transfer is consistently above chance across exact-pair and semantic evaluations (3.9–4.8× semantic chance under the CLAP-label protocol and 6.3–7.6× under independent
metadata-grounded labels). What makes it better or worse reduces to three measurable quantities:
Rit (image-text bridge quality), Rat (audio-text bridge quality), and α (projection geometry within
the modality triple and embedding dimension).
The most actionable findings concern Phase B. First, Phase B training is a necessary condition
for audio alignment: an identity ablation that skips Phase B entirely (Stage 47) yields chance
performance at all embedding dimensions, and a shuffled-caption control that randomizes Phase-B
audio-caption assignments while running the full training loop (Stage 61/W11) produces the same
collapse—establishing that semantically valid audio-caption correspondence is the specific necessary
ingredient, not the gradient updates themselves. Second, the CLAP alignment margin on Phase B
training captions—a number computable before training—predicts downstream Rat and, via the
bottleneck theory, downstream Ria ; in our observed range, higher margin is associated with stronger
bridge quality. Third, when noisy-caption data at scale (e.g., WavCaps) is unavoidable, dimension
and source composition both matter: gains to Rat can coexist with degraded Ria at high m, and
mixed noisy sources generalize worse than clean-source subsets on in-source holdout. Phase A shows
a similar quality/scale tradeoff in the W14 extension: a size-matched COCO subset can outperform
both CC3M-100K and COCO-full at higher dimensions. Centroid-gap regularization also provides a
practical post-training lever, with significant Ria gains at m = 256 and m = 512. In the main paper
we keep deployment implications concise; extended operational guidance and condition-by-condition
interpretation are provided in Appendix Q.
A full second-triple AVCaps grid and a pre-registered third-triple SpeechCoco grid provide stronger
external consistency checks for the same theory form under sharply different regimes. SpeechCoco is
especially informative as a boundary condition: with CLAP HTSAT on speech audio, α collapses
to ≈ 0.020 and Ria remains near chance at m = 512, consistent with encoder-domain mismatch
dominating architecture and dimension effects in this setting (Elizalde et al. 2022)—though a
counterfactual with a speech-trained encoder would be required to confirm this attribution. Broad
cross-modality generalization remains open. The geometric-mean form is theoretically motivated

shared low-level statistics or direct coupling. Extending this to additional triples (e.g., depth-textimage and thermal-text-image) is the next decisive test of scope.

This work is primarily methodological: it studies when modular multimodal extension can reduce
retraining cost and data-coupling requirements. Potential positive impacts include lower compute
burden for modality extension, clearer failure diagnostics before expensive retraining, and easier
deployment in settings where direct cross-modal pairing is limited. Risks include over-reliance
on text-mediated transfer in domains where encoder/domain mismatch is severe (as shown by the
SpeechCoco boundary condition), which can produce confidently wrong retrieval behavior if not
audited. Accordingly, we recommend explicit reporting of bridge-quality diagnostics, domain-shift

Causal Controls and Emergence Diagnostics

Phase B training is the causal gate. To verify that audio alignment is learned during Phase B
and does not arise from structural pre-alignment between CLAP features and the frozen COCO
embedding space, we ran an identity ablation (Stage 47): Phase A is loaded from the Stage 44
checkpoint and Phase B training is skipped entirely (epochs_phase_b=0), leaving the audio head
at random initialization. At all embedding dimensions (m ∈ {64, 128, 256, 512}), both Rat and
Ria from the identity ablation are indistinguishable from chance (≤ 0.0015) and from a raw cosine
baseline using unprocessed CLAP features (Rat = 0.0014, Ria = 0.0011). This establishes Phase B
training as a necessary condition for audio alignment.
Shuffled-caption control (W11/W12). A stronger causal control randomizes Phase-B audiocaption assignments while keeping the same training loop, optimizer, and epoch budget active
(Stage 61; 5 seeds, m = 512). This collapses Rat to 0.00118 ± 0.00028 and Ria to 0.00130 ±
0.00026—indistinguishable from the Stage 47 identity ablation—while COCO image-text recall
remains stable, confirming that the collapse is specific to semantic bridge corruption. Repeating the
shuffled-caption control at m = 64 and m = 256 (W12; 2 seeds each) again yields chance behavior
(Rat ≈ 0.001, Ria ≈ 0.0005).

Framework B: Cauchy–Schwarz factorization ceiling. Under text-mediated factorization,
matched embeddings satisfy zi ≈ Wi zt and za ≈ Wa zt (up to residuals), with unit-normalized
embeddings. For matched triples,
p
|⟨zi , za ⟩| ≤ ⟨zi , zt ⟩⟨za , zt ⟩,
which gives a geometric-mean ceiling on composed similarity. With monotone similarity-to-recall
mapping, this induces the recall-level form of Eq. 1. In this view, α is the empirical efficiency relative
to the factorization ceiling; methods with smaller centroid gap (Appendix G) achieve larger α.
Alternative forms. Three natural alternatives are min(Rit , Rat ) (hard bottleneck chain), (Rit +
Rat )/2 (arithmetic mean), and Rit · Rat (raw product). The arithmetic mean predicts that a strong leg
can offset a weak leg, contradicted by the WavCaps scaling result where weakening Rat degrades Ria
multiplicatively. The raw product underestimates observed recall by a near-constant factor, captured
by α.
Failure modes of the theory. The independence assumption can break when bridge legs share
strong low-level statistics (e.g., speech-like modalities strongly correlated with text), where α may
shift outside the observed range. Within-condition shards with near-constant Rit (e.g., sharingfactorial shards) also yield low within-shard Pearson correlation; the theory is intended to explain
variance across conditions where bridge quality changes.
Scope of the theory claim. Both frameworks above motivate the geometric-mean form and interpretation of α, but neither is presented as a formal proof that Eq. 1 must hold exactly for all retrieval
distributions. Accordingly, we treat Eq. 1 as an empirically validated scaling theory with explicit
assumptions, strengthened by held-out predictive validation (Section 6).
SDPI diagnostics (estimator-sensitive support). An aggressive MI-space diagnostic suite
(Stages 48–54) re-estimates the bridge relationships on 435 condition groups using three estimators (KSG, CANCOR proxy, Gaussian log-det). Estimator behavior is heterogeneous: SDPI-style
upper-bound violation rates are 24.1% (KSG), 0.0% (CANCOR), and 1.15% (Gaussian). Correlation
between MI-space and recall-space transitivity is strong for CANCOR/Gaussian (r = 0.902/0.854
for corr(MIia , Ria )), but weak for KSG (r = 0.159), consistent with high-dimensional nonparametric estimator noise. Across all estimators, MI-space efficiency αI is negatively correlated with
recall-space α (r ∈ [−0.52, −0.41]), so we report SDPI as a mechanistic upper-bound lens rather
than a direct derivation of Eq. 1.

yields the more fair comparison for per-unique-clip baselines; the full-pool numbers are the primary
reported metric for consistency with our own ablations.
AudioCLIP comparison. AudioCLIP [Guzhov et al., 2022] is evaluated on the 883-item 1K
overlap subset (items with both matching audio files and image frames available). Our modular linear
probe achieves Rat = 0.541 vs. AudioCLIP’s 0.297—a 1.82× advantage on audio-text retrieval—
while reaching only 38% of AudioCLIP’s image-audio performance (Ria = 0.129 vs. 0.335). The
inversion is informative: AudioCLIP is trained with direct image-audio supervision (image-audio-text
triplets), which builds a strong image-audio bridge but a comparatively weak audio-text bridge. Our
protocol inverts this: Phase B optimization over audio-text pairs builds a strong text anchor while the
image-audio channel is fed only through text-mediated transitivity. Applying
the bottleneck theory,
√
AudioCLIP’s Rat = 0.297 and Rit = 0.191 would predict Ria ≈ α̂ 0.297 × 0.191 = 0.238α̂;
with our fitted α ≈ 0.27, this predicts only 0.064—far below AudioCLIP’s observed 0.335. The
discrepancy confirms that AudioCLIP’s high Ria is not text-mediated transitivity but direct joint
supervision that bypasses the bottleneck theory entirely.
